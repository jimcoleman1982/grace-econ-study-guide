## Module 5 (Ch 20 & 21): International Trade

**Why do nations import or export certain goods?**

Nations trade because of comparative advantage. A country exports goods it can produce at a lower opportunity cost than other countries, and imports goods that other countries can produce more cheaply. By specializing in what they're relatively best at and trading for the rest, both countries end up with more total goods than if each tried to produce everything on its own.

---

**How can you show an exporting country or an importing country using supply and demand?**

On a standard supply and demand graph, draw the domestic supply and demand curves, then add a horizontal line at the world price. If the world price is above the domestic equilibrium price, domestic producers want to sell more than domestic consumers want to buy at that price, so the country exports the surplus. If the world price is below the domestic equilibrium, consumers want more than domestic producers will supply at that price, so the country imports the difference. The gap between domestic quantity supplied and domestic quantity demanded at the world price is the amount exported or imported.

---

**What are the net gains from trade, and how can we show these graphically?**

Free trade increases total surplus (consumer surplus + producer surplus) compared to the no-trade equilibrium. In an importing country, consumer surplus grows by more than producer surplus shrinks, creating a net gain. In an exporting country, producer surplus grows by more than consumer surplus shrinks, also creating a net gain. Graphically, the net gain from trade is the triangle between the domestic supply curve, the domestic demand curve, and the world price line. This triangle represents value that only exists because of trade.

---

**Who are the winners and losers with free trade?**

In an importing country, consumers win because they get lower prices and more goods, while domestic producers lose because they sell less at a lower price. In an exporting country, producers win because they sell more at a higher price, while consumers lose because they pay more. The key point is that in both cases, the winners gain more than the losers lose, so trade creates a net benefit for the country as a whole.

---

**How do countries restrict trade?**

Countries restrict trade primarily through tariffs (taxes on imported goods that raise their price), quotas (legal limits on the quantity of a good that can be imported), and non-tariff barriers such as strict regulations, licensing requirements, government subsidies to domestic industries, and voluntary export restraints. All of these tools protect domestic producers from foreign competition by making imports more expensive or harder to bring in.

---

**Given a tariff graph, explain all areas. Compared to an import graph with no tariff, how do the areas change after the tariff is implemented?**

Start with a free-trade import graph showing consumer surplus (the large triangle above the world price and below the demand curve) and producer surplus (the small triangle below the world price and above the supply curve). When a tariff is added, the effective price rises from the world price to the world price + tariff. Consumer surplus shrinks because consumers pay more and buy less. Producer surplus grows because domestic firms sell more at the higher price. The government collects tariff revenue equal to the tariff amount times the quantity still imported. Two deadweight loss triangles appear: one on the production side (domestic firms producing units they're less efficient at) and one on the consumption side (consumers who would have bought at the lower price but no longer do). The total loss to consumers is greater than the combined gain to producers and the government, and that leftover loss is the deadweight loss.

---

**Who wins/loses when a tariff is implemented? Are tariffs efficient?**

Domestic producers win because they sell more at a higher price. The government wins by collecting tariff revenue. Domestic consumers lose because they pay higher prices and buy fewer goods. Tariffs are not efficient because the loss to consumers is greater than the combined gains to producers and the government. The difference is deadweight loss, which represents economic value that is destroyed and captured by nobody.

---

**What are the arguments to restrict trade? What are some counter-arguments economists use?**

Common arguments for restricting trade include: protecting infant industries that need time to grow, national security (not wanting to depend on foreign countries for critical goods), saving domestic jobs, preventing dumping (foreign firms selling below cost to destroy competition), and leveling the playing field against countries with lower labor or environmental standards. Counter-arguments from economists include: trade restrictions raise prices for all consumers, they invite retaliation from trading partners, they protect inefficient industries instead of letting resources flow to more productive uses, and job losses in one sector are typically offset by job gains in export industries. Most economists agree that the overall gains from free trade outweigh the losses.

## Module 6 (Ch 10 & 16): International Finance

**What is a trade surplus or trade deficit?**

A trade surplus occurs when a country's exports exceed its imports, meaning more money flows in from selling goods abroad than flows out from buying foreign goods. A trade deficit is the opposite: imports exceed exports, so the country is buying more from the world than it sells. The United States has run a trade deficit for decades, meaning Americans buy more foreign goods than foreigners buy American goods.

---

**What are the accounts in the balance of payment system?**

The balance of payments has two main accounts. The current account tracks the flow of goods, services, investment income, and transfer payments in and out of the country. This is where trade surpluses or deficits show up. The capital (financial) account tracks financial capital flows, such as foreign investment coming into the country and domestic investment going abroad. The critical rule is that the two accounts must balance: a current account deficit (trade deficit) must be matched by a capital account surplus (net inflow of foreign investment), and vice versa. If we buy more goods than we sell, foreigners must be using that extra money to invest in our country.

---

**Is having a trade deficit necessarily a bad thing?**

Not necessarily. A trade deficit means the country imports more than it exports, but the flip side is that foreign capital is flowing into the country (capital account surplus). This foreign investment funds business expansion, infrastructure, and economic growth. A trade deficit can actually signal a strong economy that attracts investment from around the world. However, persistent large deficits do mean growing foreign debt, and the country must eventually pay returns on that foreign investment, so it is worth watching over the long run.

---

**Explain the different types of exchange rate policies.**

A floating exchange rate means the currency's value is determined entirely by supply and demand in foreign exchange markets with no government intervention (like the US dollar). A soft peg means the government intervenes in currency markets to keep the exchange rate near a target value but allows some fluctuation around it (China has historically used this approach). A hard peg means the government fixes the currency at a specific exchange rate and commits to maintaining it, often through a currency board that backs every domestic dollar with foreign reserves (like Hong Kong's dollar pegged to the US dollar). A merged currency is when multiple countries abandon their individual currencies and adopt a single shared currency (like the euro used across the Eurozone).

---

**What determines exchange rates and why are they sometimes volatile?**

Exchange rates are determined by supply and demand for currencies, which are driven by several factors: relative interest rates (higher rates attract foreign investment, strengthening the currency), relative inflation rates (higher inflation weakens a currency), trade balances, economic growth rates, political stability, and investor speculation. Exchange rates can be volatile because expectations about all of these factors change rapidly with new information, and speculative trading amplifies short-term swings. A surprise interest rate announcement or political event can cause large, fast currency movements.

---

**Is it always better to have a strong currency?**

No. A strong currency has trade-offs. On the positive side, it makes imports cheaper for domestic consumers and makes traveling abroad less expensive. On the negative side, it makes a country's exports more expensive for foreign buyers, which hurts domestic exporters and manufacturers competing with cheaper imports. A weaker currency does the opposite: it helps exporters sell abroad but makes imports more costly for consumers. There is no universally "better" position. The ideal depends on the country's economic priorities at that moment.

## Module 7 (Ch 7): Economic Growth and Productivity

**Potential GDP and the Production Possibilities Curve**

Potential GDP is the maximum output an economy can produce when all of its resources (labor, capital, land, and technology) are fully and efficiently employed. The Production Possibilities Curve (PPC, also called the PPF) is a graph showing the maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce with its current resources. A point on the curve represents full employment and efficiency (this corresponds to potential GDP). A point inside the curve means the economy is underperforming (like during a recession). A point outside the curve is currently unattainable without more resources or better technology.

---

**How do you push the PPF outward? Determinants of economic growth**

The PPF shifts outward when an economy increases its productive capacity, meaning it can now produce more than before. The key determinants of economic growth that push the PPF out are: increases in physical capital (more factories, machines, and infrastructure), increases in human capital (more education, training, and skills in the workforce), technological advances (new inventions and better production methods), and growth in the labor force (more workers). Investment in any of these areas expands what the economy is capable of producing.

---

**Define productivity: Real GDP per hour of labor (or more simply, output per worker); how does a country improve productivity?**

Productivity measures how much output a worker produces per hour of work, calculated as Real GDP divided by total hours worked. It is the single most important driver of rising living standards. A country improves productivity by: investing in physical capital (giving workers better tools and equipment), investing in human capital (educating and training workers), adopting new technology (innovations that make production more efficient), and improving institutions and infrastructure (better legal systems, roads, and communication networks that reduce waste and friction in the economy).

---

**Understand that Real GDP relates to economic growth, but Real GDP per capita relates to the standard of living**

Real GDP measures the total value of all goods and services an economy produces, adjusted for inflation. It tells you the size of the economic pie. Real GDP per capita divides that total by the number of people, telling you the average slice per person. A country's Real GDP can be growing (economic growth), but if the population is growing even faster, Real GDP per capita falls and the average person is actually worse off. The standard of living depends on output per person, not just total output. That is why economists focus on Real GDP per capita when comparing how well people in different countries are actually living.

---

**What is the "Rule of 72" (or sometimes called the Rule of 70)? Make a calculation.**

The Rule of 72 is a simple formula for estimating how many years it takes for something to double: divide 72 by the annual growth rate. For example, if a country's GDP grows at 3% per year, it will double in roughly 72 / 3 = 24 years. At 6% growth, it doubles in 72 / 6 = 12 years. This rule shows why small differences in growth rates are hugely important over time. A country growing at 2% doubles its output in 36 years, while a country growing at 4% doubles in just 18 years. Over a lifetime, that gap becomes enormous.

---

**Preconditions for economic growth: Economic Freedom, Property Rights, Markets**

For an economy to achieve sustained growth, three foundations need to be in place. Economic freedom means people can start businesses, engage in transactions, and keep the profits they earn without excessive government interference. Property rights mean ownership (including intellectual property like patents) is protected by law, so people have an incentive to invest, build, and create since they know they can keep or sell what they produce. Functioning markets mean prices are free to rise and fall based on supply and demand, which signals where resources should flow. Without these preconditions, people lack the incentive and ability to invest, innovate, and take productive risks.

---

**Implied public policy: what can the government do to spur long-term economic growth?**

Government can promote long-term growth by: investing in education and job training (building human capital), investing in infrastructure like roads, bridges, and broadband (building physical capital), funding basic research and development (spurring technological progress), maintaining a stable legal system with strong property rights enforcement, keeping taxes and regulations at levels that encourage private investment without being burdensome, promoting free trade to expand markets, and running sound monetary and fiscal policy to keep the economy stable and predictable. The central idea is that government's role is to create conditions where the private sector can invest and innovate most effectively.

---

**What are some negative aspects associated with economic growth?**

Economic growth can bring environmental problems like pollution, resource depletion, and climate change as production increases. Growth can increase inequality if the gains are not shared evenly across the population. Rapid growth can displace workers as technology replaces jobs or industries move overseas. There is also an opportunity cost: investing resources to grow the economy (saving, building capital) means consuming less today, which is a real trade-off. Finally, growth can bring stress, longer work hours, and disruption to communities and traditional ways of life. These downsides do not mean growth is bad, but they are real costs that societies must manage.

## Module 8 (Ch 11): Aggregate Supply and Aggregate Demand

**Explain the basics of the model: Potential GDP, AS, AD. What could shift each of these?**

The AS/AD model graphs the overall economy with the price level on the vertical axis and Real GDP on the horizontal axis. There are three key elements. Potential GDP is a vertical line at the output level where all resources are fully employed. Aggregate Demand (AD) slopes downward, showing that when the overall price level is lower, people buy more total output. Aggregate Supply (AS) slopes upward in the short run, showing that firms produce more when prices rise. AD shifts right (increases) with more government spending, tax cuts, increased consumer or business confidence, more money supply, or higher net exports; it shifts left with the opposite. Short-run AS shifts right (increases) with lower input costs, better technology, or less regulation; it shifts left with higher input costs (like an oil price spike), supply disruptions, or more regulation. Potential GDP shifts right over time as the economy gains more labor, capital, technology, or productivity.

---

**Interpret a graph to identify a recessionary gap or inflationary gap; know what fiscal (Congress) or monetary (The Fed) policy tools could be used in each situation to affect Aggregate Demand, and hence, the major macro data measurements (i.e. GDP growth, inflation, and unemployment).**

A recessionary gap exists when the equilibrium (where AS and AD cross) is to the left of Potential GDP. The economy is producing below its capacity, unemployment is high, GDP growth is sluggish, and inflation is low. To close it, Congress can use expansionary fiscal policy (increase government spending or cut taxes) and/or the Fed can use expansionary monetary policy (lower interest rates or increase the money supply). Both shift AD to the right, boosting GDP, lowering unemployment, but potentially raising inflation. An inflationary gap exists when equilibrium is to the right of Potential GDP. The economy is overheating, unemployment is very low, and inflation is rising. To close it, Congress can use contractionary fiscal policy (cut spending or raise taxes) and/or the Fed can use contractionary monetary policy (raise interest rates or decrease the money supply). Both shift AD to the left, cooling inflation but potentially slowing GDP growth and raising unemployment.

---

**Using the model, know why inflation occurs; Know the difference between demand-pull (i.e. normal inflation) and cost-push inflation (i.e. stagflation).**

Demand-pull inflation happens when Aggregate Demand shifts to the right (increases), meaning there is too much spending chasing too few goods. This is considered "normal" inflation that comes alongside a growing economy: GDP rises, unemployment falls, and the price level rises. The economy is doing well, just running a bit hot. Cost-push inflation happens when Aggregate Supply shifts to the left (decreases), caused by rising production costs like higher oil prices, higher wages, or supply chain disruptions. This is more dangerous because it means prices are rising at the same time that output is falling and unemployment is rising. This toxic combination of stagnation plus inflation is called stagflation.

---

**Know what stagflation is and the dilemma the Fed faces when trying to combat it.**

Stagflation is the combination of a stagnant economy (high unemployment, slow or negative GDP growth) and rising inflation happening at the same time. It creates a painful dilemma for the Fed because their normal tools work against each other in this situation. If the Fed fights inflation with contractionary policy (raising interest rates), it slows the economy further and makes unemployment even worse. If the Fed fights the recession with expansionary policy (lowering interest rates), it pumps more money into the economy and makes inflation even worse. There is no monetary policy move that fixes both problems at once, which is why stagflation is considered one of the hardest economic situations for policymakers to deal with.
